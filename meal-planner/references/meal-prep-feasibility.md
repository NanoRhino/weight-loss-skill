# Meal-Prep Feasibility & Food Storage Guide

This reference helps you design meal plans where every dish is realistic to store, reheat, and still taste good. A nutritionally perfect 7-day plan is useless if half the meals taste terrible by Day 3.

## Core Principle

**Plan the week around storage properties, not the other way around.** Assign batch-prep-friendly dishes to cook days, and schedule fresh-only dishes for days the user actually cooks or eats out. Never suggest storing a dish that doesn't hold up.

---

## Storage Tiers

### Tier A — Batch-Prep Champions (Store 3–4 days, reheat well)

These dishes **improve or hold steady** in flavor over a few days. Build the backbone of any meal-prep plan around them.

| Category | Examples (Western) | Examples (Chinese) | Examples (Japanese/Korean) | Why They Work |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Braised / stewed meats | Beef stew, chili, pulled chicken | Braised beef brisket, braised chicken legs, braised pork | Nikujaga, beef tendon stew, bulgogi | Connective tissue breaks down further; sauce penetrates; flavor deepens |
| Soups & thick stews | Minestrone, lentil soup, chicken soup | Spare rib soup, tomato beef soup, corn rib soup | Tonjiru (pork miso soup), kimchi jjigae | Liquid-based = reheats evenly; flavors meld |
| Legume dishes | Bean chili, dal, chickpea curry | Red bean barley porridge, mung bean soup | Lentil curry, braised black beans (kongjaban) | Beans/lentils hold texture and absorb seasoning |
| Curries | Thai curry, butter chicken, chickpea curry | Curry chicken, potato curry | Japanese curry rice, Korean curry | Sauce-based; flavor improves overnight |
| Roasted root vegetables | Sweet potato, carrots, beets, squash | Roasted sweet potato, roasted pumpkin | Simmered kabocha squash | Dense structure holds up to reheating |
| Cooked grains | Rice, quinoa, oats (for overnight oats) | White rice, brown rice, mixed-grain rice | Brown rice, multigrain rice (japgokbap) | Store separately from wet dishes; reheat with a splash of water |
| Marinated/braised eggs | — | Braised eggs, tea eggs | Marinated soft-boiled eggs (ajitsuke tamago), soy-braised beef and eggs (jangjorim) | Flavor penetrates over time; texture stays firm |

**Storage notes for grains:**
- Cooked rice: Refrigerate within 1 hour of cooking. Good for 3 days. Reheat with 1–2 tbsp water per cup, cover, microwave 1–2 min. Texture is slightly firmer but acceptable.
- Overnight oats: Prep up to 3 days ahead. Texture softens naturally — this is the point.
- Quinoa: Similar to rice. Stores well 3–4 days.

### Tier B — Store 1–2 Days, Acceptable Quality

These are fine for next-day leftovers but decline noticeably after 2 days.

| Category | Examples | Why They Degrade | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grilled/baked chicken breast | Grilled chicken, baked chicken breast | Dries out when reheated | Slice thin before reheating; add a splash of broth or sauce; don't overcook initially |
| Steamed/baked fish (firm varieties) | Baked cod, steamed sea bass | Slight fishy smell develops; texture dries | Best eaten same day or next day only. Choose firm-fleshed fish (cod, sea bass) over flaky types |
| Stir-fried firm vegetables | Broccoli, bell peppers, green beans | Lose some crunch but remain decent | Slightly undercook when batch-prepping so they're not mushy after reheating |
| Firm tofu dishes | Pan-fried tofu, tofu stir-fry, mapo tofu | Texture changes slightly; absorbs surrounding liquid | Mapo tofu actually stores well (sauce-based); pan-fried tofu is best same-day |
| Pasta (sauce stored separately) | Pasta + bolognese (in separate containers) | Pasta absorbs sauce and gets mushy if stored together | **Always store pasta and sauce separately.** Cook pasta 1 min under al dente for reheat |
| Hard-boiled eggs | Boiled eggs for salads or snacks | Whites become rubbery after 2+ days | Fine for 2 days; after that texture is off |

### Tier C — Make Fresh / Same-Day Only (Do NOT batch-prep)

These dishes degrade significantly when stored. Never include them as batch-prep meals.

| Category | Examples | What Goes Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| **Leafy stir-fries** | Sautéed spinach, stir-fried bok choy, stir-fried greens | Turn dark, yellow, or slimy overnight; release water; unappetizing texture and color |
| **Fried/crispy foods** | Fried chicken, tempura, pan-fried dumplings, spring rolls | Lose all crispiness; become soggy and greasy. Reheating in oven helps slightly but never restores original texture |
| **Raw salads** | Green salads, coleslaws with dressing | Wilt, get soggy; dressing breaks down greens. Undressed salad components can be prepped (washed, chopped) but must be assembled fresh |
| **Noodle soups** | Ramen, pho, beef noodle soup, sliced noodle soup | Noodles absorb all the broth and become bloated and mushy. **If batch-prepping soup noodles, store broth and noodles completely separately; cook noodles fresh each time** |
| **Sashimi / raw fish** | Sushi, sashimi, poke bowls | Food safety risk; texture degrades rapidly. Same-day only |
| **Cut fruit** | Pre-cut apple, avocado, banana slices | Oxidizes (browns), releases water, texture softens. Berries are the exception — they hold 2–3 days |
| **Eggs (soft/runny)** | Soft scrambled, poached, soft-boiled (runny yolk) eggs | Overcook when reheated; lose their characteristic texture. Hard-boiled is fine (see Tier B) |
| **Congee / porridge with toppings** | Century egg and pork congee | The congee itself stores fine (thickens — add water when reheating), but toppings (fried dough sticks, scallions) must be added fresh |
| **Dishes with crispy toppings** | Anything with croutons, fried shallots, fried dough sticks | Toppings go soggy. Store toppings separately and add when serving |
| **Creamy/dairy-based cold dishes** | Yogurt parfaits (assembled), cream-based dressings on salad | Separate, curdle, or get watery. Prep components separately |

## Designing a Weekly Plan Around Storage

### The 2-3-2 Pattern (Most Common for Mixed-Cooking Users)

A practical weekly structure for someone who cooks 2–3 times per week:

```
Sunday (Cook Day):   Batch-prep 2–3 Tier A dishes → covers Mon, Tue, Wed
Wednesday (Cook Day): Batch-prep 1–2 Tier A dishes → covers Thu, Fri
Sat/Sun:             Fresh-cook or eat out (good days for Tier C dishes — noodle soup, stir-fried greens, fried foods)
```

### Rules for Weekly Plan Assignment

1. **Batch-prep meals (Tier A) go on weekdays** — when the user is busiest and least likely to cook. These are grab-from-fridge-and-reheat meals.

2. **Fresh-cook dishes (Tier C) go on cook days or eating-out days** — leafy stir-fries, fried foods, noodle soups, and salads belong on days the user is actually in the kitchen or at a restaurant.

3. **Separate components that store differently:**
   - Curry + rice → store in separate containers
   - Pasta + sauce → store separately; cook pasta fresh if possible
   - Soup + noodles → store soup only; cook noodles fresh per serving
   - Salad + dressing → store separately; assemble when eating
   - Congee + toppings (fried dough sticks, scallions) → store separately

4. **Pair batch-prep proteins with easy fresh sides:**
   - Batch-prep: braised chicken thighs (Tier A, lasts 3–4 days)
   - Daily fresh side: microwave-steamed broccoli (takes 2 min, no real cooking)
   - This gives variety without requiring full cooking each day

5. **Eating-out days are perfect for storage-unfriendly foods:**
   - Ramen shop → noodle soup (can't be meal-prepped)
   - Sushi restaurant → raw fish (can't be stored)
   - Street food → fried items (savory crepes, fried chicken — can't be reheated well)

6. **Don't recommend the same batch-prep dish for more than 3 consecutive days.** Even Tier A dishes become monotonous. Alternate between 2–3 different batch-prep dishes per week.

## Reheat Instructions (Include in Plan When Relevant)

When a meal-plan dish is meant to be reheated, briefly include the recommended method:

| Method | Best For | Quick Instruction |
|---|---|---|
| Microwave + splash of water | Rice, grains, braised meats, curries | Add 1–2 tbsp water, cover, 1.5–2 min |
| Microwave (no water) | Soups, stews | Cover loosely, 2–3 min, stir halfway |
| Stovetop reheat | Stir-fries, sauced dishes | Medium heat, 2–3 min, add tiny bit of oil or water |
| Oven/toaster oven | Roasted vegetables, baked proteins | 350°F/180°C, 5–8 min (restores some texture) |
| Don't reheat — eat cold | Hard-boiled eggs, overnight oats, some grain bowls | Some foods are designed to be eaten cold |

## Ingredient-Level Freshness Notes

### Proteins
| Ingredient | Fridge Life (Raw) | Fridge Life (Cooked) | Freezer Life | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Chicken breast/thigh | 1–2 days | 3–4 days | 2–3 months | Thigh stores/reheats better than breast (more fat = less drying) |
| Ground meat (beef/turkey/pork) | 1–2 days | 3–4 days | 2–3 months | Cook within 1–2 days of purchase |
| Fish (fresh) | 1–2 days | 1–2 days | 1–2 months | Buy on the day you plan to cook it, or buy frozen |
| Shrimp | 1–2 days | 2–3 days | 3 months | Frozen shrimp is often fresher than "fresh" counter shrimp |
| Eggs | 3–5 weeks | 1 week (hard-boiled) | Not recommended | Hard-boiled: peel before storing for convenience; unpeeled lasts longer |
| Tofu (opened) | 3–5 days (in water, change daily) | 3–4 days | 2 months (texture changes) | Frozen tofu becomes spongy — good for absorbing sauce, bad if you want smooth texture |

### Vegetables
| Ingredient | Fridge Life (Raw) | Best Storage Method | Batch-Prep Friendly? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Broccoli, cauliflower | 5–7 days | Unwashed, in loose bag | Yes — blanch or roast, stores 3–4 days cooked |
| Leafy greens (spinach, lettuce, romaine) | 3–5 days raw | Wrap in paper towel, sealed container | **No** — cook and eat same day; wilts and discolors when stored cooked |
| Bell peppers | 1–2 weeks | Whole in crisper drawer | Okay — roasted peppers store well; raw sliced ones dry out |
| Carrots, celery | 2–3 weeks | Submerged in water in container, or in bag | Yes — very resilient raw and cooked |
| Mushrooms | 5–7 days | Paper bag, not plastic | Moderate — sautéed mushrooms store 2–3 days but release water |
| Tomatoes | 5–7 days (ripe) | Counter until ripe, then fridge | Yes for sauces/stews; raw sliced tomatoes get mushy |
| Cabbage (regular cabbage, napa cabbage) | 1–2 weeks | Whole in crisper | Yes — sturdy; holds up well in stews, stir-fries, and even reheated |
| Root vegetables (potato, sweet potato, pumpkin) | 2–4 weeks | Cool, dark, dry place | Yes — roasted or steamed, stores very well 3–4 days |

### Fruits
| Ingredient | Counter Life | Fridge Life | Prep-Ahead Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Apples | 1 week | 3–4 weeks | Don't pre-cut (browns). Eat whole. |
| Bananas | 3–5 days | Fridge slows ripening (skin browns but fruit is fine) | Pre-cut for smoothie bags (freeze) — don't refrigerate cut |
| Berries | 1 day | 3–5 days | Best fruit for meal prep — rinse, dry, store in single layer |
| Citrus (oranges, mandarins) | 1 week | 2–3 weeks | Peel fresh; don't pre-segment (dries out) |
| Avocado | 2–4 days (until ripe) | 3–5 days once ripe | Cut avocado: squeeze lemon/lime, press plastic wrap on surface. Lasts 1 day tops |
| Grapes | — | 1–2 weeks | Wash only what you'll eat (moisture promotes mold) |

## Common Mistakes to Avoid in Meal Plans

1. **Scheduling leafy-green stir-fries as Tuesday's batch-prep for Wednesday/Thursday.** Stir-fried greens, stir-fried spinach, and stir-fried romaine are terrible reheated — they turn dark and slimy. Use them only on days the user cooks fresh. If you need vegetables for batch-prep days, use broccoli, root vegetables, cabbage, or frozen mixed vegetables instead.

2. **Recommending fried foods for meal prep.** Pan-fried dumplings, fried chicken, tempura — these rely on crispiness. They become soggy after storage. Use steamed or braised alternatives for prep days (steamed dumplings instead of pan-fried, braised chicken instead of fried).

3. **Putting noodle soup in a prep container for the next day.** The noodles absorb all the broth and become a swollen, textureless mass. If the plan includes noodle soup, it must be a fresh-cook or eat-out meal. If the user wants to prep soup, store broth and noodles separately with a note to cook noodles fresh.

4. **Suggesting fish for Day 3 of a batch prep.** Most cooked fish tastes noticeably worse after 2 days — the fishy smell intensifies. Fish dishes should be eaten same-day or next-day at most. For batch-prep proteins, use chicken thigh, beef, pork, or tofu instead.

5. **Pre-cutting fruit for the whole week.** Cut apples brown, cut avocado turns gray, cut bananas get mushy. Only berries and citrus segments hold up. Recommend whole fruits as snacks — they're already grab-and-go.

6. **Storing assembled salads with dressing.** The dressing wilts the greens within hours. If salad is in the plan, note that dressing must be stored separately and added fresh. Better yet: schedule salads for eating-out or fresh-prep days.

7. **Ignoring that cooked rice needs proper handling.** Cooked rice left at room temperature grows Bacillus cereus. Cool and refrigerate within 1 hour. Reheat thoroughly. Don't store more than 3 days. Include these notes when rice is a batch-prep staple.

8. **Making one giant container of stir-fry for the week.** Even Tier A dishes get monotonous after 3 days. Prep 2–3 different dishes and rotate. Sunday → Dish A (Mon-Tue) + Dish B (Tue-Wed); Wednesday → Dish C (Thu-Fri).
